Three frameworks for
customer success in

I built this after spending time studying how CSMs operate in technical B2B infrastructure companies — specifically the parts that require original judgment rather than product knowledge: how to evaluate account health before a customer tells you something is wrong, how to structure your first 90 days so you’re owning accounts rather than shadowing them, and how to run a client relationship so problems reach you while they’re still small. These are my frameworks, not summaries of anyone else’s.

02

30-60-90 day CSM playbook

A personal framework for moving from learning to owning in a technical CSM role

1
Days 1–30 — learn without performing
The instinct to contribute early is the biggest mistake. Resist it.
Learn ▾
A CSM who helps before they understand gives bad advice and loses credibility fast. The first 30 days are for building the foundation that every customer interaction will sit on — not for impressing anyone.
Go through the entire product as a customer — not a demo, an actual end-to-end experience. Note every moment of friction and every assumption the product makes about the user’s workflow.
Read the API documentation completely. Understand what data is returned, what each endpoint does, and where customers historically get stuck — before you’re on a call where they ask.
Shadow at least 10 customer calls across different segments. Write down every question customers ask twice. Patterns in repeated questions are usually unmet onboarding needs, not customer confusion.
Map the portfolio before touching it: high-value accounts, at-risk accounts, expansion candidates, and accounts that haven’t reached their contracted use case yet.
Meet Engineering, Product, and Sales separately. Ask each: “Where does the handoff from you to CS usually break down?” The answers will differ. That delta is where your job actually lives.
Define what “healthy” looks like per customer segment — usage benchmarks, time-to-first-value, active user thresholds — before inheriting accounts so you have a baseline to evaluate against.

2
Days 31–60 — contribute with a safety net
Own accounts with support. Debrief every call. Build your first internal deliverable.
Contribute ▾
The goal isn’t to prove you can do the job — it’s to do the job in a way where mistakes are recoverable. Take ownership of 2–3 accounts, run your first full onboarding, and debrief with your manager after each one.
Take ownership of 2–3 active accounts. Start with a discovery call that treats the relationship as new even if it isn’t — ask what’s working, what’s not, and what they wish the product did differently.
Run a full onboarding end-to-end for one new customer: kickoff through first successful use. Document friction points. The ones you find in your first onboarding will be the same ones every new customer hits.
Build a health scorecard for your accounts using real usage data. Knowing which accounts are at risk before they are is the entire job — start building that muscle now.
Identify one pattern surfacing across multiple customer conversations and bring it to Product as a structured observation — specific, supported by more than one example, with enough context to evaluate against the roadmap.
Build or improve one internal resource: an onboarding template, a training guide for a specific segment, or an FAQ from the questions you heard most in month one. Institutional knowledge that lives only in your head is fragile.
For any account below usage target, diagnose before prescribing. Workflow friction, champion loss, technical barrier, and value mismatch each have different solutions — and the wrong solution makes it worse.
0 of 6
3
Days 61–90 — own it, don’t just manage it
Full portfolio ownership. At least one expansion conversation. Proactive, not reactive.
Own ▾
Managing accounts is answering when customers call. Owning accounts is knowing what they need before they call. By day 90 that distinction should be visible in how you run your portfolio — in the conversations you’re initiating, not just responding to.
Run full quarterly business reviews for assigned accounts. Lead with their ROI data — specific to their numbers, not industry averages — before any conversation about the product or the contract.
Identify at least one genuine expansion opportunity per account. Not an upsell — an expansion that makes the customer more successful. The distinction matters in how you pitch it and whether it sticks.
Build a proactive monitoring cadence using health signals. The accounts you check in on before they contact you are the ones that renew. The accounts you only hear from when there’s a problem are the ones that churn.
Deliver one meaningful piece of customer intelligence to Product — not a feature request, a pattern. “Three customers in the same segment use the override feature for the same reason” is actionable. “A customer wants a new feature” is not.
Document each account: workflow, key contacts, version of success, known risks, open threads. Account knowledge that only exists in your head is not an asset — it’s a liability for the team.
Start the renewal conversation 60 days before contract end. Lead with their ROI story. A renewal conversation that begins with the contract date is already late.
0 of 6
The north star metric for any technical infrastructure CSM
Usage rate per agreement is the only number that can’t lie. A customer at 95% is getting value and will renew. A customer at 40% has a problem that needs diagnosing before it becomes a churn conversation you’re losing.
03

The trust-and-diagnosis loop

How I build client trust, find the real issue, and bring in internal teams without losing ownership

1
Earn trust before you need it
Trust isn’t built during a crisis — it’s spent during one.
Trust ▾
The relationship is an account you make deposits into during the calm periods so there’s a balance to draw on when something breaks. A CSM who only appears when there’s a problem has no credibility at the moment credibility matters most.
—
Be useful before you’re asked. Send the benchmark, the workaround, the relevant release note — small, unprompted value is what makes a customer pick up the phone later.
—
Do what you said, by when you said. Reliability on small commitments is what earns you the benefit of the doubt on the large ones.
—
Say “I don’t know yet, I’ll find out” instead of bluffing. A customer who catches you guessing once will discount everything you say after it.
—
Learn the customer as people, not a logo — who’s under pressure internally, who the real decision-maker is, what a win looks like for them personally.
2
Diagnose the real issue, not the reported one
The reported problem and the real problem are rarely the same sentence.
Diagnose ▾
Customers describe symptoms in the language of their frustration. “The product is slow” might mean a real latency issue, a training gap, or a workflow the product was never built to support. Prescribing before you’ve diagnosed is how you lose trust and time at once.
—
Ask what they were trying to accomplish when the problem came up — the goal behind the complaint is usually the thing that actually needs solving.
—
Separate the technical fact from the business impact. “Verifications take eight seconds” and “my staff are skipping the step” are different problems with different fixes.
—
Ask what they’ve already tried. It shows you how they think about the product and stops you from suggesting the thing that already failed.
—
Restate the problem in your own words and get an explicit “yes, that’s it” before proposing anything. Half of all misalignment dies right here.
—
Sort it into a category — workflow friction, training gap, technical barrier, value mismatch, or champion change. Each one routes to a different next step.
3
Route it — know what’s yours and what isn’t
Owning an account means making sure it gets solved, not solving all of it yourself.
Collaborate ▾
The fastest way to lose an internal team’s goodwill is to forward them a frustrated customer email and call it a handoff. Internal collaboration is a skill: you make the other team’s job easy, and you stay accountable for the outcome even after it leaves your hands.
—
Bring Engineering a reproducible case — steps, expected vs. actual, account context — not a forwarded complaint. You’re the translation layer between customer language and technical detail.
—
Bring Product a pattern, not a one-off. “Three accounts in the same segment hit this” gets traction; “my customer wants X” gets filed.
—
Give Sales the context before the customer does. A renewal or expansion conversation goes better when Sales already knows where the relationship actually stands.
—
Never hand off silently. The customer should hear what’s happening from you first — staying the single point of contact is what keeps you the account owner.
—
Commit to a realistic timeline, not a hopeful one. An “update by Thursday” you can keep beats a “soon” you can’t.
4
Close the loop — credibility compounds
The handoff is done when the customer has the answer, not when the internal team picks it up.
Return ▾
Every loop you close cleanly makes the next trust deposit cheaper. Every loop you leave open — even with a perfectly good internal reason — the customer files as “I had to chase them.” This last step is where the trust from step one gets renewed or quietly spent down.
—
Come back even when the answer is “not on the roadmap.” A clear no, delivered by you, beats a silence the customer has to interpret.
—
Translate the internal response into their language and their impact — what it means for their workflow, not what the ticket said.
—
Write down what you learned on the account record — the issue, the resolution, the open threads. It’s how the relationship survives you being out for a week.
—
Then go back to step one. The customer who watched you close a loop well is the one who tells you about the next problem while it’s still small.
Why the loop is the actual job
A customer who trusts you tells you about the problem while it’s still small. That early warning is the entire advantage of the role — and you only get it if the relationship was built before the problem existed.
